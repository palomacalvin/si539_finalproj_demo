Michigan Mammal Conservation

Welcome to the Michigan Mammal Conservation Field Guide! This webpage documents some mammal conservation efforts in the state of Michigan. The goal of this site is to give people a broad overview of conservation efforts, threatened species, identification, and ecological change over time.

The information on these pages is non-exhaustive but is a starting point to learn more about Michigan's mammal ecology, historical distribution, and conservation efforts.

Conservation Efforts

Conservation efforts have a long history, but federal and state protections largely began in the mid-1970s, starting with the Endangered Species Act in 1973 and Michigan's state version, Public Act 203, in 1974. Since then, many organizations and community members have contributed to the protection of Michigan's most vulnerable mammal species, as well as a variety of other animal groups.

Eventually, Public Act 203 was recodified and became part of the Natural Resources and Environmental Protection Agency Act 451 of 1994 as Part 365, Endangered Species Protection.

These acts help to define vulnerable species, create funding for their research and conservation, and develop programs to execute conservation efforts.

These efforts undertaken by organizations at various levels have resulted in greater research and understanding about different animal groups. This means that, over time, more plans and animals have been added to Michigan's list of vulnerable species. However, conservation efforts are equally as strong, and many species have been downgraded from Endangered to Threatened, or removed from vulnerable species lists entirely.

One example of this is the Smokey Shrew, which is now considered a species of Special Concern rather than a Threatened species.

Conservation Organizations

There are quite a few organizations that take part in mammal conservation in Michigan.

  • The Michigan Department of Natural Resources (DNR). The Michigan DNR mananges mammal conservation across the state through initatives and planning programs as well as their wildlife rehabilitation permitting and wildlife offices. The DNR keeps is the main institution keeping track of threatened and endangered species, and planning conservation efforts through the Michigan Wildlife Action Plan, Forest Management for Michigan Wildlife Plan, wildlife permits, and more.
  • The Michigan Natural Features Inventory (MNFI) through Michigan State University is an organization that manages and conducts research around Michigan's biodiversity. MFNI provides authoritative information about biodiveristy in Michigan to support conservation efforts across the state through programs and services provided by the MSU Extension. MFNI also conducts field surveys to collect and manage high-quality data on rare plants and animals found in the state. MFNI also hosts the Michigan Natural Heritage database.
  • U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (Michigan Field Office) administers the Endangered Species Act, manages wildlife resources, and restores contaminated habitats. It also acts as a mediator to protect wildlife during major construction projects, in particular public resources such as roads and bridges. The Fish and Wildlife Service works across animal groups, protecting and enhancing the habitat of birds, fish, mammals, and plants.
  • University of Michigan Museum of Zoology (UMMZ) supports conservation efforts as a research institution through field work, preservation, and research. The museum hosts over 150,000 specimens which are used for critical ecological, biodiversity, and health research.
  • Michigan Nature Association (MNA) is an accredited non-profit organization that seeks to protect Michigan's threatened wildlife through conservation and land stewardship.
  • The Nature Conservancy (Michigan Chapter) is an offshoot branch of a global environmental nonprofit that does critical work around ecological restoration and protection, animal conservation and wildlife, and more. The organization's priorities are to tackle climate change; protect ocean, land, and fresh water; and provide food and water.
  • Detroit Zoological Society is the organization that supports the Detroit Zoo and Belle Isle Nature Center. The Detroit Zoological Society seeks to directly aid conservation efforts and save threatened species across the globe.

This list is non-exhaustive, and there are many other individuals, communities, and groups that contribute to keeping Michigan's mammals safe.

How You Can Help

There are many ways that you can contribute to protecting Michigan's mammals.

  1. Volunteer or donate to organizations that support Michigan's wildlife.
  2. Work with wildlife rehabilitation specialists in your spare time.
  3. Protect the land and nature where you live, work, and spend your free time.
  4. Hunt and fish responsibly. Follow local ordinances and keep up to date with your license.
  5. Participate in citizen science projects like the Michigan DNR’s ‘Eyes in the Field’ to report specific sightings of diseased wildlife, rare species, or nesting activities.
  6. Create wildlife-friendly habitats in your own backyard by planting native Michigan flora. Avoid using pesticides and rodenticides, which can move up the food chain and harm predatory mammals like foxes and hawks.
  7. Practice responsible coexistence. Secure trash cans and bird feeders to prevent mammals from becoming habituated to human food, which often leads to conflict and the removal of the animal.
  8. Support local land use policies that prioritize wildlife corridors and green spaces, ensuring that animal populations aren't isolated by highways or urban sprawl.
  9. Educate your community. Share what you’ve learned about Michigan’s threatened species to help foster a culture of conservation and respect for our state’s natural heritage.
