Moose

Alces alces
Status: Special concern
Estimated Population: 426 (2023)

According to the Michigan DNR, moose are native throughout Michigan, found even in the Lower Peninsula before the late 1800s. Now, populations are scarce in both the Lower and Upper Peninsula, with population declines largely attributed to logging and mining, as well as the expansion of deer habitats which are common hosts for a parasite deadly to moose.

Gray wolf

Canis lupus
Status: Special concern
Estimated Population: 768 (2024)

According to the Michigan DNR, wolves have been members of the Great Lakes Region since the melting of the last glacier and are considered native to Michigan. They are considered mythological symbols in many regional indigenous groups, and were once seen across the entire state, in both the Lower and Upper peninsula. However, extreme wolf bounties throughout the 1800s and 1900s led to severe population declines across the United States, with most wolves now elimiated from Michigan. However, small populations exist in the Upper Peninsula to this day, supported by stable populations in Canada and northern Minnesota.

Least shrew

Cryptotis parvus
Status: Threatened
Estimated Population: Unknown

One of Michigan's smallest shrews, not to be confused with rodents, is considered threatened in the state of Michigan, although its status is of Least Concern at a federal level. Once populous in Southern Michigan, populations have since declined because of "natural phenomena, genetic mutations, and/or intensive land use by humans" (MNFI). Preferring plains, prairie, and grassy upland habitats, land use changes in Southern Michigan may have contributed to these declines.

Current population levels are unknown, as surveys for the least shrew are infrequent.

Cougar

Puma concolor
Status: Endangered
Estimated Population: Unknown

Once native across the state, cougars were largely eliminated. Small populations still exist in the Upper Peninsula, with record numbers of cougar sightings occuring in 2025. These large cats prefer minimal contact with humans.

Although a current self-sustaining population is unknown in the state, in 2025 Michigan's first cougar cubs were born for the first time in over 100 years.

Northern flying squirrel

Glaucomys sabrinus
Status: Special concern
Estimated Population: Unknown

The northern flying squirrel inhabits both the Lower and Upper Peninsulas. The species is rare, with uncertain and limited natural habitats. Studies have shown that the Northern flying squirrel's territories have shrunk over time, while the southern flying squirrel populations have begun to expand into areas previously inhabited by the northern flying squirrel (pa.gov). The northern flying squirrel relies on large areas of undisturbed forest, areas of which have been declining in the state for decades.

Canada lynx

Lynx canadensis
Status: Endangered
Estimated Population: Unknown

The Canada lynx is a medium-sized cat. Currently, there are no stable breeding populations in the state of Michigan, which rare sightings occurring in Isle Royale and nearby areas. However, most of these sightings come from stronger breeding populations in Minnesota and Canada (U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service). The species is threatened by illegal poaching and loss of mature coniferous forests (MNFI).

Prairie vole

Microtus ochrogaster
Status: Endangered
Estimated Population: Unknown
Photo by Jordan Schmidt, iNaturalist (CC-BY-NC).

The prairie vole is a unique vole with interesting social structure, in that these voles pair with a mate for life and are monogamous (MI Wildlife Conservancy). This type of reproductive behavior may contribute to its population declines, since the animals are so short-lived. As of 2015, the prairie vole has been classed as Endangered in Michigan, resulting from habitat loss. The vole had not been documented for years until 2006, where it was discovered in Kalamazoo County (MI Wildlife Conservancy).

Woodland vole

Microtus pinetorum
Status: Special concern
Estimated Population: Unknown

According to the Michigan Natural Features Inventory, the woodland vole is has scattered populations throughout the Lower Peninsula of Michigan, with potential to occur throughout the entire state. The woodland vole relies heavily on leaf cover and primarily lives in deciduous forests. Land use changes may affect the species' populations.

Indiana bat

Myotis sodalis
Status: Endangered
Estimated Population: Unknown
Photo by: Amiel Hopkins, iNaturalist (CC-BY-NC).

The Indiana bat is considered critically endangered in the state of Michigan. Its status as a vulnerable species is also federally recognized. According to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, population insecurity largely stems from winter hibernation habitat disturbance by humans, particularly through the commercialization of cave systems that support their livelihood. Furthermore, pesticides and white-nose syndrom persistently threaten populations. Currently, the Indiana bat can primarily be found in the Southern Lower Peninsula, with some bats living farther north into Michigan's thumb. As of 2019, population estimates across the bats' range in the United States were a total of 537,297 individuals.

Evening bat

Nycticeius humeralis
Status: Threatened
Estimated Population: Unknown
Photo by JPage, iNaturalist (CC-BY-NC).

The Evening bat is considered threatened in the state of Michigan. It is a tree-roosting, insectivorous bat. Similar to the Indiana bat, its populations have been significantly affected by pesticide use, white-nose syndrome, and habitat loss.

Eastern pipistrelle

Pipistrellus subflavus
Status: Special Concern
Estimated Population: Unknown
Photo by Phillip T. Crawford,
iNaturalist (CC-BY-NC).

The Eastern pipistrelle, typically referred to as the Tri-Colored Bat, is a threatened species that is considered critically imperiled. The uniquely-colored bat has suffered extreme population declines due to white-nose syndrome, with populations decreasing 90% according to the Michigan Nature Association.

Smoky shrew

Sorex fumeus
Status: Threatened
Estimated Population: Unknown
Photo by rkluzco, iNaturalist (CC-BY-NC).

The smoky shrew is classed as threatened, and is considered critically imperiled in the state of Michigan. It is extremely rare in the state, requiring ample leaf litter to sustain populations. It is only known to live in specific parts of the Upper Peninsula, particularly areas of higher elevation (MNFI).
